Yes, an undergraduate degree in Business (Marketing) meets the admission requirements for the UTS Master of Information Technology.

According to the official UTS Master of Information Technology program page (https://www.uts.edu.au/study/find-a-course/master-information-technology), admission is open to applicants with a bachelor’s degree in **any field of study**, including Business (Marketing). For those who do not meet the direct entry criteria, UTS offers a Graduate Certificate in Information Technology Studies (C11257), which allows for recognition of prior learning (RPL) and can lead to up to 24 credit points being transferred into the Master of Information Technology.

## Q4: What are the English language proficiency requirements for the UTS and UNSW Master of Information Technology programs? For an applicant with average English proficiency, which program would be the more advisable primary application target?

The English language proficiency requirements for the University of Technology Sydney (UTS) and University of New South Wales (UNSW) Master of Information Technology programs are as follows:

---

### **University of Technology Sydney (UTS) Master of Information Technology**

- **IELTS Academic**:  
  - Overall band score: 6.5  
  - Writing: minimum 6.0  

- **TOEFL iBT (0–120 scale)**:  
  - Overall: 79  
  - Writing: 21  

- **TOEFL iBT (1–6 scale)**:  
  - Overall: 4  
  - Writing: 4.5  

- **Pearson PTE Academic**:  
  - Overall: 58  
  - Writing: 50

  - Overall: 176  
  - Writing: 169  

- **UTS College AE5**:  
  - A pass is required (no specific writing minimum)  

> **Note**: All test results must be current and dated within **two years** of the intended start date of the program.

---

### **University of New South Wales (UNSW) Master of Information Technology**

- **IELTS Academic**:  
  - Overall band score: 6.5  
  - Each subtest (Reading, Writing, Listening, Speaking): minimum 6.0  

- **TOEFL iBT**:  
  - Overall: 90  
  - Writing: ≥23  
  - Reading, Listening, Speaking: ≥22  

- **Pearson PTE Academic**:  
  - Overall: 64  
  - Each subtest: ≥54  

- **Cambridge C1 Advanced**:  
  - Overall: 176  
  - Each subtest: ≥169  

- **Cambridge C2 Proficiency**:  
  - Overall: 180  
  - Each subtest: ≥180  

> **Important notes**:  
> - All test results must be dated within **two years** of the intended start date.  
> - **TOEFL MyBest scores are not accepted**.  
> - **TOEFL new format is not accepted**; applicants must register for the "TOEFL for Australia" version.  
> - Applicants who completed Year 12 in Australia **do not need** to provide English proficiency evidence.  
> - Applicants who do not meet the requirements may qualify through UNSW College Academic English Programs.

---

### **Comparison and Recommendation for Applicants with Average English Proficiency**

While both programs require a minimum IELTS 6.5 overall, **UNSW has stricter and more consistent subtest requirements**—requiring a minimum of 6.0 in every component (reading, writing, listening, speaking) for IELTS, and higher individual thresholds in TOEFL and PTE. For an applicant with average English proficiency, this means:

- The **UTS requirements are slightly more lenient** in writing (e.g., 21 in TOEFL vs. 23 in UNSW), but the overall structure still demands a solid command of academic English.
- The **UNSW program’s requirement for all subtests to meet minimum thresholds** reflects a higher academic standard, which may be more aligned with the demands of a technical master’s program.
- **UNSW offers a structured progression pathway** (Graduate Certificate in IT + Master of IT), allowing applicants to build foundational skills before advancing—ideal for those with average English and limited technical background.
- The **UNSW Master of IT is accredited by the Australian Computer Society (ACS)**, enhancing graduate employability and providing a clear pathway to professional certification—valuable for career-oriented students.

In contrast, UTS does not mention such accreditation, and while it offers Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) for up to 24 credit points, this is primarily for work experience and does not directly support language or technical skill development for beginners.

Given these factors—particularly the structured entry path, academic rigor, and professional accreditation—**UNSW is more advisable as a primary application target** for an applicant with average English proficiency.

## Q9: There appears to be a discrepancy between the student visa requirements stated by the Department of Home Affairs and those stated by the university. Which source should be considered the authoritative reference?

**Timestamp:** 2026-05-31 14:15:59
**Knowledge Base:** Curated markdown knowledge base (UTS_Master_of_IT.md, UNSW_Master_of_IT.md)

**System Response:**
The **Australian Department of Home Affairs** is the authoritative source for student visa requirements in Australia.

While universities such as the University of New South Wales (UNSW) and the University of Technology Sydney (UTS) provide important program-specific information—such as eligibility for post-graduation work opportunities or course duration—they do so within the context of their own admission policies and program structures. This information may support a student’s eligibility for a visa pathway, but it is not the primary or legally binding source of visa rules.

In contrast, the Department of Home Affairs is the government body responsible for legislating and administering Australian visas, including the **Student Visa (Subclass 50 or 500)** and the **Temporary Graduate Visa (Subclass 485)**. Its official website (https://immi.homeaffairs.gov.au) contains the definitive, legally binding requirements, conditions, and eligibility criteria for these visas. These include:

- The specific eligibility criteria for graduates to apply for a Subclass 485 visa  
- The validity periods of the visa based on study location (e.g., regional vs. metropolitan)  
- The required documents and application processes  
- Updates and changes to visa rules over time  

Therefore, when assessing the official, legally binding requirements of a student visa, the Department of Home Affairs should be considered the primary and authoritative reference. Universities can offer valuable guidance on program outcomes and pathways, but they do not set or interpret the legal conditions of the visa itself.
